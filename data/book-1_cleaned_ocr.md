#1: The Golden Rule 
Recognize what is in your control - impressions, reactions - and what is not: externals. Have the right idea and you will be proof against harm.

#2: Desire & Aversion
If desiring and you fail -> unhappy.
If you experience something you're avoiding -> unhappy.
Restrict yourself to choice (of aversions) and refusal (of desires); exercise self-discipline and detachment.
- Cognizance of impermanence & death.

#3: Reasonable Handling of Attachments & Delights
If you like something (object), say "I am fond of it."
For Nadia, Emily, people, say "I am using / embracing."

#4: Mental Prep in Advance of Planning / Doing
"I wish to [do X], but I also want to keep my will in line with nature."
Say this everytime before doing.

#5: Events Don't Disturb; Judgments Do
Blaming others is ignorance. Blaming yourself is progress. "But the wise man never has to blame another or himself."

#6: Pride
Don't be proud of your assets. Only pride in mastering mental faculties.

#7: The Ship is Calling
Prepare to leave everything behind in a moment.

#8: Hope (see #12)
Don't hope, just accept; welcome events as they come.

#9: Sickness
Is a body problem, not the mind. Unless you decide it is.

#10: Cope: Turning Discomfort to Acceptance
You will discover your strength to cope through experience, withstanding things, and reflection.
- Withstand, reflect, reintroduce.

#11: Impermanence & Death
Say not that "I lost something," but "I returned it."
- See: Seneca, Marcus Aurelius on Nature's cycle.
- See: Behavioral, Path of Grief and/or Resentment.

#12: Be Minimal, Free of Grief and/or Resentment

#13: Humility (Resenting Nature)
Put up with being perceived as ignorant / unaware of worldly matters. "If you impress others, don't believe it." - Epictetus

#14: Slave = Control
Do not be a slave to other things, needs, or people.

#15: Divinity
Don't let your desires get the best of you.

#16: Against Empathy (Paul Bloom)
Sympathize, don't commiserate.

#17: Actor in a Play
Director decides. See your role, accept it.

#22: Don't aspire for a reputation of sagacity - keen judgment, acute discernment. Securing whose effect should always be kept in mind.

#23: Don't seek approval from the outside as it compromises integrity.

#29: Contemplate Projects before Starting
Otherwise we start with enthusiasm, and when we encounter difficulty, we quit. Tasks require discipline, restraint. Don't emulate a monkey who imitates what it sees, infatuated with task after task without ever really committing.

#33: Who do you want to be? Commit.
- Let silence be your goal - say only what is necessary, and be brief. Laugh minimally.
- If indulging, only in limit libations.
- Someone speaking ill of me? Don't defend. Say: "Yes, and he doesn't know the half of it, otherwise he would have mentioned more."

Summary of Enchiridion:
Good advice, but Epictetus preaches ultra-orthodox Stoicism - don't covet, commit all your effort and lived experience to Philosophy & be in service to the craft.

Transcendental Idealism & Critique of Pure Reason

His Project: The possibility of metaphysics in a priori knowledge that does not depend on experience. To examine whether, how, and to what extent human reason is capable of a priori knowledge.

His Copernican Revolution:
Just as Copernicus revealed that bodies don't revolve around the observer, but that a dual consideration of perspective is necessary, in metaphysics our intuition must conform to the objects - but instead, objects must conform to our a priori constitution of our faculty of intuition.

Transcendental Aesthetic & Logic:
- Space & time are our pure a priori intuitions allowing us to be cognizant of the sensible world.

Transcendental Idealism:
- Human beings experience only appearances, not things in themselves.
- Space & time are not things in themselves (are transcendentally ideal).
- They are nothing other than the subjective forms of human sensible intuition.
- They are empirically real: "everything that can come before us externally as an object" is in both space & time, and our internal intuitions of ourselves are in time.

Distinguishes between two classes of objects:

Two standpoints on the objects of experience:
- Empirical aspect (objects as experienced)
- Transcendental aspect (objects as they are in themselves, independent of our cognitive faculties).
Conforms to epistemic conditions peculiar to human cognitive faculties.

The meat and potatoes of the Critique, and the most complex & difficult text in philosophical history. Shows how concepts can be known in themselves.
